Do LLMs dynamically add new knowledge?

Curious how LLMs, like GenAI assistants, update knowledge during interactions?

Suppose a user asks a question, but the information isn't part of the pre-trained datasets or fine-tuned knowledge the model has absorbed.

The LLM doesn't initially know the answer. However, it can navigate the internet and find up-to-date information to respond.¬†But can the LLM internally absorb this new knowledge and make it available for future queries?

The short answer is no.

LLMs are built with fixed neural network weights after pre-training and fine-tuning, meaning any new information retrieved during a session is ephemeral‚Äîit‚Äôs used for that session only and forgotten afterward.

To address this limitation, approaches like Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) are gaining traction. RAG dynamically fetches and integrates external knowledge (e.g., from databases or the web) during response generation without needing to modify the model itself.

Still, this is a form of dynamic retrieval, not long-term absorption. For permanent knowledge updates, external processes such as incremental fine-tuning or memory-augmented architectures would be required.

Without these, the LLM remains stateless, dependent on temporary retrieval mechanisms like RAG to supplement static knowledge in real-time.

What is Synthetic Data?

Synthetic data is artificially generated information used to train AI models, including large language models (LLMs).

Instead of collecting real-world data from human interactions, researchers create this data using algorithms, simulations, or even other AI models. It mimics real data patterns but avoids privacy concerns and biases from actual human data.


LLMs trained by thousands of humans.

LLM models mirror both Internet data and human feedback.

While synthetic data helps reduce dependency on human-generated content, AI companies like xAI (makers of Grok) and other major AI labs still employ thousands of human trainers for several critical purposes:


1. Data labeling and annotation: Human workers categorize and tag information to help AI recognize patterns correctly.


2. Preference ranking: Trainers evaluate and rank different AI responses to the same query, helping models learn which outputs are more helpful or appropriate.


3. Constitutional AI training: Humans review AI responses against ethical guidelines to ensure the system behaves responsibly.


4. RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback): Trainers provide feedback on model outputs, which is converted into reward signals that help the AI learn what humans consider valuable.


5. Red-teaming: Specialized trainers deliberately test AI systems for weaknesses, biases, or harmful outputs.


These companies typically structure their workforce with a small core of highly-specialized AI researchers and engineers, supported by much larger teams of contractors or gig workers who perform the labor-intensive training tasks. Many of these workers are recruited through specialized vendors or platforms that manage large distributed workforces across different regions and languages.


The quality and diversity of these human trainers significantly impact how well AI systems understand cultural nuances, ethical considerations, and human preferences.
